ARTIST'S STATEMENT

Nature evoked in a photograph is always quiet since the wind in the leaves, the sound of the rain, and the chorus of birdsong can only be imagined. The photographs in "Silence and Flux" envision this silence yet nature is never really still for, as the ancient Greek philosopher Heraclitus wrote, "Everything changes and nothing remains still." Though we view our natural surroundings in color during the day, I've sought to convey the moods and effects and mystery of this "silence and flux" in nature by printing this group of photographs in various shades of sepia and black and white. Using archival pigment-based ink, I print each photograph individually with the use of archival Japanese Washi Unryu paper and thick archival watercolor paper. I find that by using this muted color palette, printing process, and paper in my work, I am able to achieve prints that blur the line between photography and other art forms. Aside from the series "Flux 1-5," which is printed on thick archival Exhibition Watercolor paper, the photographs in this exhibition have been printed on thin, almost transparent (if held up to light) Japanese Washi Unryu paper, which also has nature running through it as Washi paper is a traditional Japanese paper made from the fibers of the paper mulberry plant, which are soaked in clear river water, thickened, and then filtered through a bamboo screen. Very resilient, Washi paper is said to last for a thousand years. Washi Unryu paper, "Cloud Dragon Paper," made by adding long swirling hemp fibers to a wet layer of Kozo (mulberry) fiber, adds an abstract windy texture to the finished prints. The images in "Silence and Flux" were taken locally in the Catskills in the New Kingston hamlet and valley and along the edge of the Pepacton reservoir, in New York City's Central Park and Gramercy Park, and on Martha's Vineyard off Cape Cod. While some of the images are more representative, others edge into abstraction. All were taken in spring and summer at various times of day.
